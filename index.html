For the past few years, I thought I was designing incentives. In reality, I was solving a harder question: where should the next dollar go to create the most long-term value?

What problem have I actually been solving?

My work sat across growth, monetisation and creator incentives. On the surface, the tools were missions, rewards, subsidies, creator progression and revenue programs.

But underneath, every decision came back to the same marketplace question:

Given a fixed budget, which users should we invest in, which behaviours should we encourage, and how do we know the investment created incremental value?

How I spent money: what a $3M budget taught me

Budget ownership
$1M → $3M

The budget under my ownership expanded because programs demonstrated incremental ROI. The goal was not to spend more; it was to prove where additional spend deserved to go.

Long-term return
0.98x → 2.2x

Some programs looked weak in the first window, but delivered strong long-term return through retention, monetisation and creator progression.

Behaviour change
0.2% → 5%

Completion improved by changing target selection, task design, feedback loops and incentives — not simply by increasing reward size.

Lesson #1: Not all growth is equal.
Growth without incrementality is simply spend.
Lesson #2: Short-term metrics can lie.
The objective is durable behaviour change, not temporary activity.
Lesson #3: Not everyone is worth subsidising.
The most important allocation decision is often deciding who not to invest in.

If I joined Wolt: how I would approach Consumer Pricing

Many pricing discussions start with: what price should we charge?

I would start with: what behaviour are we trying to create?

Because pricing is not only about revenue capture. It shapes ordering frequency, Wolt+ retention, courier utilisation, marketplace density, ETA reliability and long-term contribution.

1. Who is worth defending?

Segment customers by LTV, price sensitivity, Wolt+ status, order habit and churn risk. The priority is high-LTV users an entrant can actually tempt.

2. Where is spend worth it?

Clear the market locally, by zone and time slot. Subsidy should go where it protects density, not where it only buys low-retention volume.

3. When do we stop?

Set kill criteria before launch. If retention or frequency does not hold after subsidy ends, the spend created a bubble, not value.

My working thesis for Wolt

Do not blanket-match a subsidising entrant. Defend the high-LTV core, protect Wolt+ retention, preserve courier density, and let low-retention price-only volume go.

External case: what China’s food-delivery battle taught me

China made the same allocation problem more extreme: several well-funded platforms used consumer discounts, merchant subsidies and courier incentives at the same time. Reuters reported that Alibaba, JD.com and Meituan pledged $28bn in instant-retail subsidies in 2025, while S&P Global analysts later estimated at least RMB 160bn of spending over 12–18 months to defend or grow share.

The important learning was not that subsidies are useless. The learning was that subsidies only matter if they convert into retained frequency, better density, or structural unit-economics advantage.

How I would actually take data, calculate, and decide

A. Start with a cohort-level P&L view

I would avoid judging pricing or subsidy decisions only on gross order growth. I would first build a cohort-level contribution view:

Cohort LTV = margin per order × order frequency × expected lifetime
Incremental value = LTV with intervention − LTV without intervention
Decision rule = invest while marginal subsidy ≤ incremental LTV protected

This is the same logic I used in incentive allocation: the spend is justified only if it creates incremental long-term value.

B. Read elasticity by segment, not as one average

A single platform-level elasticity number is too blunt. I would calculate response curves by customer cohort, city, zone, time slot and order occasion.

C. Build a marketplace-aware pricing model

Food delivery pricing cannot be optimised only at customer level. The model needs to know the local operational state.

Subject to: ETA floor, fulfillment floor, courier supply, budget cap, regulatory guardrails

Inputs: customer LTV, price elasticity, Wolt+ status, distance, basket size, courier utilisation, merchant density, competitor pressure
Output: price / fee / subsidy / no-action decision by cohort × zone × time

D. Test through holdouts and rotational experiments

In my own work, I used holdouts, A/B testing, significance testing, sample-size estimation and rotational experiments to avoid acting on noise. For Wolt, I would apply the same discipline:

Holdout design

Keep comparable customers, zones or time windows untreated so we can separate true incrementality from organic trend.

Rotation design

Rotate pricing or subsidy treatment by zone-hour where operationally feasible, especially for food delivery where local supply-demand balance changes quickly.
