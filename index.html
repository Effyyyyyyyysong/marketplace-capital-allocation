00Executive summary

Three things I believe about consumer pricing in marketplaces

Before the analysis, the operating philosophy is simple: consumer pricing is not a coupon function or a fee-setting function. It is the capital-allocation layer of a three-sided marketplace.

Belief 01

Pricing is capital allocation.

The real decision is not “what price should we charge?” but “which behaviour is worth buying, defending, or letting go?”

Belief 02

Share can be bought. Density cannot.

A competitor can subsidise demand quickly. It is much harder to replicate courier density, merchant depth, subscription habit and service reliability.

Belief 03

Optimise lifetime value, not order margin.

The best price is sometimes not the one that maximises the current order; it is the one that grows retained frequency and downstream profit pools.

01The origin

I did not start from pricing. I started from a simpler question: why does the same dollar work differently on different users?

For a long time, I thought I was doing growth. Campaigns, missions, incentives, subsidies, creator progression. But when you manage real budget at scale, the job quickly becomes less about launching activity and more about deciding which behaviour is worth buying.

What changed the way I think?

Two programs can spend the same amount. One creates a spike that disappears. The other creates retained behaviour.

That is when growth stops being a campaign problem and becomes a capital-allocation problem.

The real question was never “how much incentive should we give?”
It was: who is worth investing in, what behaviour are we buying, and how do we know it would not have happened anyway?
02What I learned spending money

I was not spending budget. I was buying behaviour.

Across a 1.6M creator base, I managed incentive allocation to roughly 16,000 creators. My budget grew from ~$1M to ~$3M not because we spent more aggressively, but because the system became more measurable: holdouts, A/B tests, significance checks, sample-size planning, and rotational experiments.

Why this started to feel like pricing

Incentives and pricing look different on the surface. One gives money out; the other asks users to pay. But both are trying to shape behaviour under constraints. Both need a view of elasticity, incrementality, LTV and budget. Both can destroy value if they subsidise users who would have acted anyway.

03Profit anatomy

Then I studied food delivery — and found the delivery fee does not really make the money.

Food delivery is one of the cleanest examples of marketplace capital allocation. It has demand, supply, merchants, price, subsidy, retention and operational constraints — all clearing locally by zone and time. The surprising part is that the delivery transaction itself is usually engineered close to break-even. The profit comes from the layers above it.

The structure underneath

Profit lives in three layers above delivery

Delivery is the transaction layer. Its job is to feed higher-margin layers that a competitor cannot subsidise away overnight.

Advertising
Highest margin. Retail media and sponsored placement monetise app frequency and purchase intent.
Quick commerce
Basket and frequency. Grocery / retail / multi-vertical use cases turn a food app into an everyday app.
Subscription
Retention lock-in. Wolt+ / DashPass pre-commits demand and is hard for a subsidising entrant to dislodge.
Delivery
The courier / merchant / payment layer. It must be reliable, dense and efficient — but it is not where most durable margin lives.
04What this means for Wolt

Consumer Pricing should not optimise the order. It should optimise the portfolio.

If Wolt’s profit pools are ads, Wolt+ and quick commerce, pricing should be judged on whether it grows those pools — not only whether the individual delivery order makes money.

Wolt Ads

Retail media monetises frequency and intent. Pricing that increases profitable frequency has value beyond order margin.

Wolt+ subscription

Subscription members are the retention core. A defensive budget should protect this base first.

Q-commerce

Grocery and retail raise basket, frequency and cross-vertical lifetime value.

Density

Dense zones convert demand into better ETA, utilisation and repeat behaviour.

Pricing implication

Sometimes the right price loses a little at the order line because it buys a high-margin habit above it. But only if the habit is real, retained, and incremental.

05What price wars actually teach

Price wars are not about who can buy the most orders. They are about who can convert bought orders into density, habit and margin.

China’s delivery war and Keeta’s overseas expansion are useful because they show both failure modes: match every subsidy and destroy margin; refuse to match but fail to defend the moat and lose the market.

Two failure modes in subsidy wars

Strategic read

Match everything

Order share may hold, but the category gives away the entire margin pool.

Refuse without a moat

Discipline alone fails if service, selection, subscription and supply are not strong enough.

Theatre 1 — domestic instant-retail war
Not a fight about food. A fight to own the dense fulfilment network behind the everyday app.

The same formula, exported: attack supply, discount demand, then monetise after share is locked.
ATTACK
The entrant attacks both sides of the market

Free delivery flips price-sensitive demand. Higher courier pay degrades incumbent service. Merchant switching accelerates the flywheel.

DEFENCE
Refusal alone is not strategy

Do not blanket-match. But refusing to match only works if the incumbent has something stronger to defend: density, subscription, merchant depth and service reliability.

densitysubscriptionmerchant depth

How the entrant attacks

Keeta-style playbook
  1. Starve supply first. Poach couriers so incumbent orders are slower and less reliable.
  2. Flip price-sensitive demand with free delivery and deep coupons.
  3. Pry loose merchants where exclusivity is weak or switching costs are low.
  4. Fund losses long from a deep parent balance sheet.
  5. Monetise after winning through commission or take-rate expansion once share is locked.

What actually defends

Incumbent options
  1. Protect courier supply before defending price.
  2. Lock retention in subscription — the asset a coupon cannot replicate quickly.
  3. Deepen merchant relationships through selection, tools and advertising value.
  4. Do not blanket-match. That is the Meituan trap. But refusal alone is the Deliveroo trap.
  5. Defend selectively — high-LTV / Wolt+ core and dense zones first.
The Wolt thesis

Between two failure modes, one disciplined path

Match every subsidy and margin dies. Refuse and do nothing else and the market can still be lost. The right defence is targeted: protect density, Wolt+ retention, merchant depth and high-LTV customers while letting mercenary volume walk.

Market share can be bought. Density, subscription lock-in and merchant trust cannot.

06If I were Director

I would run Consumer Pricing as a capital-allocation system.

The decision is not “raise or lower price.” The decision is: which behaviour to buy, which cohort to defend, where density is worth protecting, and how to know when to stop.

The operating model

Maximise long-term contribution(cohort, zone, time)
Subject to: ETA floor, fulfillment floor, courier supply, budget cap, regulatory guardrails

Inputs: customer LTV, price elasticity, Wolt+ status, distance, basket size, courier utilisation, merchant density, competitor pressure
Output: price / fee / subsidy / no-action decision by cohort × zone × time
SituationWhat I would do firstDecision rule
Competitor launches free delivery Separate high-LTV / Wolt+ users from low-retention deal seekers; read churn, frequency and basket response. Defend only where subsidy protects incremental LTV or subscription retention.
Courier supply gets attacked Watch ETA, cancellation, unpicked orders and courier utilisation by zone-hour before focusing only on demand price. Protect supply density where service degradation threatens high-LTV demand.
Order volume spikes from subsidy Run holdout / rotation read to separate organic growth, seasonality, competitor effect and pricing impact. Scale only if post-subsidy frequency holds.
Budget is fixed Build defend / monitor / cede matrix by LTV and price sensitivity. Do not fund the bottom-right cell: low-LTV, high price-sensitive users.
